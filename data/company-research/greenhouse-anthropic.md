# Anthropic — research
Provider/key: greenhouse:anthropic | company_type: product

What they do: Anthropic is an AI safety company and Claude AI developer. They build large language models (Claude family: Claude 3, Claude 3.5, Claude 3.7 Sonnet, etc.) and focus on AI safety research (Constitutional AI, interpretability, scalability). Products include Claude.ai (consumer/API), Claude for Enterprise, and the Claude API. Growing revenue, rapid headcount expansion. One of the most prominent AI labs globally alongside OpenAI and Google DeepMind.

How they describe themselves: Seven guiding principles: "Act for the global good," "Hold light and shade" (both risks and benefits of AI), "Be good to users," "Ignite a race to the top on safety," "Do the simple thing that works," "Be helpful, honest, and harmless," "Put the mission first." Mission: safe and beneficial AI development for humanity. Culture: "engineers do lots of research, and researchers do lots of engineering" — blurred boundaries between research and engineering. High expectations for technical depth and safety-consciousness.

Size / stage / funding: ~3,000+ employees (~392 open roles as of mid-2026, rapid growth). Founded 2021 by Dario Amodei, Daniela Amodei, and team from OpenAI. Total funding: $10B+ (including Google, Amazon investments). Valuation: ~$61B (2024). Revenue growing rapidly (reportedly $1B+ ARR in 2024). HQ: San Francisco, CA. Offices: NYC, Seattle, London, Dublin, Bangalore, Seoul, Tokyo.

Locations / HQ: San Francisco CA (HQ, primary office), New York City NY, Seattle WA, London UK, Dublin IE, Bangalore IN, Seoul KR, Tokyo JP, Zürich CH, Washington DC. No Canadian office.

Remote policy: Office-first. Most staff are in the Bay Area; expected to come to the office regularly. ~7-8% of open roles are listed as "remote-friendly." Remote staff expected to visit office ~1 week/month ("remote-friendly with travel required"). Policy explicitly: "Anthropic does not offer remote work from countries where it doesn't have an entity." No Canadian entity.

Remote-Canada eligibility: No — confirmed. Anthropic explicitly does not hire in Canada for remote roles because they have no Canadian entity. The policy "does not offer remote work from countries where it doesn't have an entity" is confirmed by independent research (jobsbyculture.com, 2026). Canadian locations appear only in the Anthropic Fellows Program (academic partnership), not regular engineering employment. Infrastructure, data engineering, and security roles are all SF/NYC/Seattle only, with some "Remote-Friendly (Travel-Required)" options for US-based candidates.

Engineering & tech: ML infrastructure at massive scale — training runs for frontier models, inference optimization, GPU cluster management, sandboxing/isolation, safeguards engineering, security engineering, data platform. Specific teams: Inference Systems, ML Infrastructure (Safeguards), Sandboxing/Infrastructure, Claude Code (model performance), Detection & Response Platform (security), Economic Research Data Platform. Stack: Python-heavy, JAX/PyTorch for ML, cloud infrastructure on AWS/GCP, Kubernetes. Significant investment in inference performance and safety tooling.

Notable / other: One of the most technically exciting AI safety companies. The engineering work is genuinely interesting (frontier model infrastructure). However, the strong office-first culture and no-Canada-entity policy makes this a hard non-starter for remote-Canada candidates. The Anthropic Fellows Program does have Ontario, Canada presence but that is academic, not employment. Rapid growth trajectory with no IPO timeline announced. Employee compensation reportedly $300k–$759k total (Sonnet 4.6 / engineer levels).

Open relevant roles (sample): Analytics Data Engineer; Data Engineer, Safeguards; Infrastructure Engineer, Sandboxing; ML Infrastructure Engineer, Safeguards; Model Performance Software Engineer, Claude Code; Performance Engineer, Inference Systems; Research Engineer, Economic Research Data Platform; Security Software Engineer, Detection & Response Platform.
